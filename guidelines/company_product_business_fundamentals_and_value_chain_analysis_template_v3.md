# Company Product & Business Fundamentals Analysis Template

> A reusable framework for understanding what a company sells, why customers buy it, how durable its advantage is, what could go wrong, and what the business may become.

---

## 1. Purpose and Scope

This framework is designed for **bottom-up qualitative company due diligence**. It should be completed before—or alongside—detailed valuation work.

The objective is to answer:

> Is this a valuable product inside a sound business, operated by capable management, with a durable reason to win?

This framework can be used for:

- Public-equity research
- Private-company diligence
- Competitor analysis
- Product and industry research
- Investment memos
- Interview preparation
- Strategic planning

---

## 2. Evidence Standards

### 2.1 Source hierarchy

Use sources in roughly this order of reliability:

| Tier | Source type | Typical examples | How to use it |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Regulatory and legally accountable disclosures | 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, proxy statement, prospectus, regulator databases | Establish facts, risks, ownership, financials, contracts, compensation, litigation |
| 2 | Direct operational evidence | Product documentation, pricing pages, customer contracts, usage data, app-store data, procurement records | Understand what the product does and how customers use or buy it |
| 3 | Independent primary research | Customer interviews, former employee interviews, distributor checks, expert calls, product testing | Validate product quality, switching costs, sales process, and competitive claims |
| 4 | Credible third-party analysis | Industry research, trade publications, specialist analysts, academic papers | Add market context and technical understanding |
| 5 | Company-controlled narrative | Investor presentations, earnings calls, press releases, executive interviews | Understand management’s claims, priorities, and messaging |
| 6 | Informal or anecdotal sources | Social media, forums, Glassdoor, Reddit, reseller comments | Generate hypotheses; do not treat as established fact without corroboration |

### 2.2 Minimum evidence standard

For every important conclusion:

1. Separate **fact**, **management claim**, and **analyst inference**.
2. Prefer at least two independent sources for material conclusions.
3. Record the date of every source.
4. Compare management’s current claims with prior promises.
5. Seek disconfirming evidence, not just supporting evidence.
6. Quantify the claim whenever possible.
7. State confidence as **high**, **medium**, or **low**.

Recommended notation:

- **Fact:** Directly supported by a primary source.
- **Claim:** Stated by management, customers, competitors, or an expert.
- **Inference:** Your conclusion based on multiple facts or claims.
- **Open question:** Important issue not yet resolved.

# Part I — Company and Product

## 3. Company Snapshot

### Objective

Explain the company clearly enough that a reader unfamiliar with it can understand the business in one page.

### Questions

- What does the company sell?
- Who is the customer?
- What problem does it solve?
- Who pays, and who actually uses the product?
- How does the company make money?
- What are the major business segments?
- Where does it operate?
- What percentage of revenue and profit comes from each segment?
- What stage is the company in: pre-product, early adoption, scaling, mature, or declining?

### Standard for a strong analysis

A strong company snapshot should:

- Describe the company without relying on marketing language.
- Distinguish the core business from experimental or promotional initiatives.
- Identify the economic buyer, user, and beneficiary separately.
- Show revenue and profit contribution by segment.
- Explain the business model in one sentence.

### Suggested output

> **Business model statement:**  
> The company provides **[product/service]** to **[customer]**, solves **[problem]**, and earns revenue through **[pricing/revenue model]**.

### Where to find information

- Annual report or 10-K: Business section, segment reporting, geographic reporting
- Company website and product pages
- Investor-relations presentation
- Regulatory licenses and registrations
- Prospectus or S-1 for younger public companies
- Industry directories
- Company pricing and contract pages
- Competitor websites

### Red flags

- The business cannot be explained simply.
- Revenue comes from a different activity than the company’s public narrative suggests.
- Segment disclosures are repeatedly changed.
- The “core product” contributes little revenue.
- Important economics are hidden behind nonstandard metrics.

## 4. Customer Problem and Product Value Proposition

### Objective

Determine whether the product solves a real, important, and persistent customer problem.

### Questions

- What job is the customer hiring the product to perform?
- How painful is the problem?
- How often does the problem occur?
- What happens if the customer does nothing?
- Is the product mission-critical, productivity-enhancing, or discretionary?
- Does the product save money, increase revenue, reduce risk, save time, or satisfy an emotional need?
- Is the value proposition measurable?
- How quickly does the customer receive value?
- Is the product “nice to have” or “must have”?

### Standard for a strong analysis

A strong conclusion should identify:

- The exact customer pain point
- The economic or practical value delivered
- The urgency of the need
- The customer’s current alternative
- Evidence that customers recognize the value

Avoid vague statements such as “the company improves efficiency.” State how, for whom, and by how much.

### Useful metrics

- Customer payback period
- Time saved
- Cost reduction
- Revenue uplift
- Error reduction
- Downtime avoided
- Conversion improvement
- Customer satisfaction
- Net Promoter Score, used cautiously
- Product utilization
- Repeat purchase or renewal behavior

### Where to find information

- Customer case studies
- Product demonstrations and free trials
- Product documentation
- User reviews
- Procurement requests and request-for-proposal documents
- Customer interviews
- Sales-engineer and implementation-partner interviews
- Industry forums
- Competitor comparisons
- Customer earnings calls mentioning the product

### Red flags

- Customers cannot clearly explain the product’s value.
- The product requires heavy customization to work.
- Benefits are difficult to measure.
- Customers use only a small part of the product.
- Adoption is driven mainly by discounts.
- The product is easily replaced by internal tools or manual processes.

## 5. Product Quality and Product-Market Fit

### Objective

Assess whether the product works well and whether a sufficiently large group of customers strongly wants it.

### Questions

- Does the product reliably perform its core function?
- How does product quality compare with alternatives?
- What are the most common complaints?
- How fast is implementation?
- How difficult is onboarding?
- Does usage deepen over time?
- Are customers expanding their purchases?
- Are customers renewing without major discounts?
- Does the company have a clear ideal customer profile?
- Is growth driven by genuine adoption or promotional spending?

### Standard for a strong analysis

Evidence of product-market fit should include several of the following:

- Strong retention
- High repeat usage
- Organic referrals
- Expansion within existing customers
- Shortening sales cycles
- Customers willing to tolerate price increases
- A clear and consistent use case
- Low dependence on one-off customization
- Positive customer references from multiple segments

### Useful metrics

For subscription businesses:

- Gross revenue retention
- Net revenue retention
- Logo retention
- Churn
- Expansion rate
- Daily/monthly active users
- Seat utilization
- Contract duration

For consumer businesses:

- Repeat purchase frequency
- Cohort retention
- Engagement
- Purchase frequency
- Customer acquisition cost
- Contribution margin
- Refund and return rates

For industrial businesses:

- Installed base
- Utilization
- Backlog
- Warranty claims
- Service revenue
- Replacement cycle
- On-time delivery
- Field failure rates

### Where to find information

- Company disclosures
- Product analytics, when available
- App stores and review platforms
- Customer references
- Reseller and distributor interviews
- Support forums
- Implementation partners
- Job postings for customer deployment teams
- Competitor product trials
- Churn commentary in earnings calls

### Red flags

- High headline growth but weak retention.
- Customers buy pilots but do not expand.
- Reported bookings do not convert to revenue.
- Usage falls after initial adoption.
- The company avoids disclosing retention after previously doing so.
- Growth depends on free or heavily discounted offerings.

# Part II — Customers and Market

## 6. Customer Base and Customer Economics

### Objective

Understand who the customers are, why they buy, how concentrated they are, and whether serving them is economically attractive.

### Questions

- Who are the largest customer groups?
- Are customers enterprises, small businesses, governments, consumers, or intermediaries?
- How concentrated is revenue?
- How long is the sales cycle?
- Who makes the purchase decision?
- What budget pays for the product?
- How difficult is procurement?
- What is the customer acquisition channel?
- What is the cost to acquire and serve a customer?
- How long does it take to recover acquisition costs?
- Are customer relationships direct or controlled by distributors or platforms?

### Standard for a strong analysis

A strong customer analysis should:

- Segment customers by use case, size, geography, and economics.
- Identify the true decision-maker.
- Quantify concentration.
- Distinguish customer count from economically meaningful customers.
- Explain how customer economics differ across segments.
- Show whether unit economics improve or deteriorate with scale.

### Useful metrics

- Revenue by customer segment
- Top-10 customer concentration
- Average contract value
- Customer acquisition cost
- Lifetime value
- Sales efficiency
- Payback period
- Gross margin by segment
- Renewal rate
- Expansion rate
- Days sales outstanding
- Backlog and remaining performance obligations

### Where to find information

- 10-K customer-concentration notes
- Revenue recognition footnotes
- Earnings presentations
- Government contract databases
- Customer announcements
- Channel partner lists
- Distributor filings
- Procurement portals
- Customer interviews
- Sales job postings
- Industry conferences

### Red flags

- One customer represents a large share of revenue.
- Reported customer count includes inactive or trivial accounts.
- Acquisition costs rise faster than customer value.
- Sales cycles lengthen while management calls demand “strong.”
- Channel partners capture most of the economics.
- Receivables grow much faster than sales.

## 7. Market Structure and Addressable Market

### Objective

Estimate the realistic market opportunity and determine whether the industry structure supports attractive economics.

### Questions

- What market is the company actually serving today?
- What adjacent markets could it plausibly enter?
- Is the market growing, stable, cyclical, or declining?
- What drives demand?
- How concentrated are suppliers and customers?
- Are there regulatory or technical barriers?
- Is the market local, regional, or global?
- How much of the market is economically addressable?
- What share can the company realistically capture?
- Does market growth create value, or attract destructive competition?

### Standard for a strong analysis

Avoid accepting management’s total addressable market estimate without reconstruction.

Build three layers:

1. **Total addressable market:** All theoretical demand.
2. **Serviceable available market:** Demand the current product can serve.
3. **Serviceable obtainable market:** Share the company can realistically win.

Use bottom-up estimates whenever possible:

> Number of customers × realistic annual spending per customer

### Where to find information

- Government statistics
- Trade associations
- Industry shipment data
- Competitor filings
- Customer budgets
- Procurement data
- Regulatory databases
- Specialist industry research
- Import/export databases
- Channel checks
- Academic and technical publications

### Red flags

- Market size is based on unrelated categories.
- Management repeatedly expands the TAM definition.
- The company must enter several unproven adjacencies to justify growth.
- The industry grows rapidly but generates poor returns.
- Demand depends heavily on subsidies or temporary regulation.

# Part III — Competitive Position

## 8. Competitive Landscape

### Objective

Identify all meaningful alternatives available to the customer, not only companies management labels as competitors.

### Competitor categories

- Direct competitors
- Lower-cost competitors
- Premium alternatives
- Internal customer solutions
- Manual processes
- Adjacent products
- Open-source alternatives
- New entrants
- Large incumbents that could bundle the product
- The option to do nothing

### Questions

- What alternatives appear in customer evaluations?
- On what dimensions do competitors win?
- Is competition based on price, quality, trust, integration, distribution, speed, or regulation?
- Are market shares stable?
- How often do customers switch vendors?
- Who is gaining and losing share?
- Does the market tend toward consolidation or fragmentation?
- Can a larger platform bundle the product for free?

### Standard for a strong analysis

Create a competitor matrix based on customer decision criteria, not company-selected metrics.

| Decision criterion | Company | Competitor A | Competitor B | Internal solution |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Product quality |  |  |  |  |
| Price |  |  |  |  |
| Ease of implementation |  |  |  |  |
| Reliability |  |  |  |  |
| Distribution |  |  |  |  |
| Switching cost |  |  |  |  |
| Ecosystem/integration |  |  |  |  |
| Customer support |  |  |  |  |

### Where to find information

- Customer interviews
- Competitor websites and filings
- Product trials
- Pricing pages
- Industry conferences
- Request-for-proposal documents
- Reseller feedback
- Review platforms
- Search trends
- Market-share data
- Lost-deal commentary from former sales staff
- Antitrust and regulatory filings

### Red flags

- Management claims it has no competitors.
- Competitive comparisons omit the market leader.
- The product wins only through lower pricing.
- Competitors can replicate features quickly.
- A platform owner controls access to customers.

## 9. Competitive Advantage and Moat

### Objective

Determine why the company can earn attractive returns despite competition—and whether the advantage is strengthening or weakening.

### Potential moat sources

- Network effects
- Switching costs
- Cost advantage
- Scale economies
- Brand and trust
- Proprietary technology
- Proprietary data
- Distribution advantage
- Regulatory approval
- Intellectual property
- Ecosystem or developer platform
- Scarce physical assets
- Process know-how
- Long-term customer relationships
- Superior culture or execution

### Questions

- What does the company possess that competitors cannot easily buy or copy?
- How long would replication take?
- What would replication cost?
- Why have competitors not already copied it?
- Does the advantage improve with scale?
- Does the customer become more dependent over time?
- Can the company raise prices without losing customers?
- Does the advantage create measurable superior economics?
- Is the moat at the product, company, distribution, regulatory, or ecosystem level?

### Standard for a strong analysis

A moat should be supported by a causal chain:

> **Asset or capability → customer benefit → customer behavior → economic outcome**

Example:

> Deep workflow integration → high replacement effort → low churn → recurring revenue and pricing power.

A feature alone is not a moat. A patent count alone is not a moat. Market leadership alone is not a moat.

### Evidence that a moat may be real

- Sustained high retention
- Stable or rising market share
- Pricing power
- Superior gross margins
- Lower acquisition costs
- Strong returns on incremental capital
- Competitors repeatedly fail to displace the product
- Customer value increases with the installed base
- Proprietary data improves product performance

### Where to find information

- Customer interviews
- Cohort and retention data
- Pricing history
- Gross-margin history
- Market-share trends
- Patent databases
- Technical publications
- Integration documentation
- Developer ecosystems
- Distribution agreements
- Regulatory approvals
- Competitor postmortems
- Former employee interviews

### Red flags

- The “moat” is only first-mover status.
- Patent quantity is emphasized without commercial relevance.
- High margins depend on underinvestment.
- Switching costs are claimed but churn is high.
- Customer concentration gives buyers strong negotiating power.
- The advantage disappears if a supplier or platform changes terms.

# Part IV — Business Model and Economics

## 10. Revenue Model and Pricing Power

### Objective

Understand how the company converts product value into revenue and whether pricing is durable.

### Questions

- Is pricing subscription-based, usage-based, transaction-based, advertising-based, licensing-based, or project-based?
- What determines the customer bill?
- Is revenue recurring or repeatedly re-sold?
- Are contracts cancellable?
- Is demand sensitive to price?
- Does pricing scale with customer value?
- Are discounts increasing?
- Can the company raise prices?
- Does reported growth come from volume, price, mix, acquisitions, or accounting changes?

### Standard for a strong analysis

Decompose revenue growth:

> Revenue growth = customer growth + usage growth + price/mix + acquisitions + currency effects

Evaluate pricing based on realized behavior, not management intention.

### Where to find information

- Contracts and terms of service
- Pricing pages
- Revenue-recognition footnotes
- Earnings calls
- Customer invoices
- Channel partners
- Customer interviews
- Historical pricing archives
- Competitor pricing
- Procurement databases

### Red flags

- “Recurring” revenue is cancellable or project-based.
- Bookings rely on unusually long contracts with weak cancellation protection.
- Price increases cause usage reductions.
- Growth is driven mainly by acquisitions.
- Revenue is recognized well before cash collection.

---

## 11. Unit Economics and Scalability

### Objective

Determine whether each incremental customer, unit, or transaction creates value.

### Questions

- What is the contribution margin per customer or product?
- Which costs scale directly with revenue?
- Which costs are fixed?
- Does gross margin improve with scale?
- What implementation and support costs are excluded from gross margin?
- How much working capital is required?
- How capital-intensive is growth?
- Does the company need more employees in proportion to revenue?
- Are economies of scale real or merely projected?

### Standard for a strong analysis

Use the most economically meaningful unit:

- Customer
- Store
- Shipment
- Transaction
- Device
- Installed system
- Production unit
- Sales representative
- Geographic market

Calculate:

- Revenue per unit
- Variable cost per unit
- Contribution profit per unit
- Acquisition cost
- Payback period
- Incremental margin
- Capital required per unit
- Return on incremental capital

### Where to find information

- Financial statements and footnotes
- Segment disclosures
- Investor presentations
- Unit-level operating disclosures
- Franchise disclosures
- Customer contracts
- Supplier agreements
- Employee counts
- Capital expenditure disclosures
- Industry benchmarks

### Red flags

- Company-level margins hide weak unit economics.
- Growth requires increasing subsidies.
- Support or implementation expenses are classified as sales costs.
- Capital expenditures are excluded from management’s preferred cash-flow metric.
- Mature customer cohorts remain unprofitable.

---

## 12. Financial Quality and Cash Conversion

### Objective

Test whether reported growth and earnings translate into durable cash generation.

### Questions

- How does revenue convert to gross profit, operating profit, and cash flow?
- Are margins structurally improving?
- Is free cash flow supported by operations or working-capital timing?
- Are stock-based compensation and dilution material?
- Are capitalized costs aggressive?
- Are acquisitions obscuring organic performance?
- Are receivables, inventory, or contract assets rising unusually fast?
- Does the balance sheet provide resilience?

### Standard for a strong analysis

At minimum, examine five years of:

- Revenue
- Organic growth
- Gross margin
- Operating margin
- Free cash flow
- Capital expenditures
- Working capital
- Share count
- Stock-based compensation
- Debt
- Return on invested capital

Reconcile management’s non-GAAP metrics to GAAP figures.

### Where to find information

- 10-K and 10-Q financial statements
- Footnotes
- Cash-flow statement
- Revenue-recognition policy
- Debt covenants
- Credit agreements
- Auditor commentary
- Competitor financials
- Rating-agency reports

### Red flags

- Receivables or inventory consistently grow faster than revenue.
- “Adjusted profit” excludes recurring expenses.
- Stock-based compensation is treated as economically irrelevant.
- Free cash flow depends on delaying supplier payments.
- Frequent acquisitions prevent organic comparisons.
- Auditor changes or material weaknesses recur.

# Part V — Management and Governance

## 13. Management Quality

### Objective

Assess management’s competence, integrity, strategic judgment, and execution.

### Questions

- What is management’s relevant operating history?
- Has the team built and scaled similar businesses?
- Does management understand the product and customer?
- Does it acknowledge mistakes?
- Has it delivered against prior guidance?
- Does it allocate capital rationally?
- Does it communicate consistently?
- How dependent is the company on one founder or executive?
- Is senior management turnover unusually high?

### Standard for a strong analysis

Evaluate management across four dimensions:

| Dimension | Test |
|---|---|
| Competence | Can management operate and scale the business? |
| Candor | Does management discuss problems honestly? |
| Capital allocation | Does management invest, acquire, repurchase shares, and finance the company rationally? |
| Alignment | Do incentives reward long-term per-share value creation? |

Build a **promise-versus-delivery table**:

| Date | Management commitment | Target date | Actual outcome | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Where to find information

- Earnings-call transcripts
- Investor-day presentations
- Historical guidance
- Proxy statements
- Executive biographies
- Prior employers and board roles
- Acquisition history
- Insider transactions
- Former employee interviews
- Litigation and regulatory records
- Media interviews

### Red flags

- Constantly changing key performance indicators.
- Heavy promotional activity unrelated to operating progress.
- Repeated missed targets without accountability.
- Excessive related-party transactions.
- High executive turnover.
- Large compensation despite poor per-share outcomes.
- Capital allocation driven by empire building.

## 14. Governance, Ownership, and Incentives

### Objective

Determine who controls the company, how decisions are supervised, and whether insiders are aligned with outside shareholders.

### Questions

- Who owns and controls the company?
- Are voting and economic ownership aligned?
- Is the board independent and qualified?
- Are directors meaningfully invested?
- How is management compensated?
- What performance metrics drive incentives?
- Are targets easy to manipulate?
- Are there related-party transactions?
- Is dilution significant?
- Are there anti-takeover protections?
- Does a dual-class structure weaken accountability?

### Standard for a strong analysis

Explain:

- Voting control
- Insider economic ownership
- Board composition and expertise
- Compensation structure
- Dilution potential
- Related-party dealings
- Shareholder rights
- Succession planning

### Where to find information

- Proxy statement, usually DEF 14A
- Forms 3, 4, and 5 for insider ownership changes
- Schedule 13D and 13G filings
- Corporate charter and bylaws
- Compensation plans
- Related-party transaction notes
- Board biographies
- Shareholder meeting materials

### Red flags

- Compensation rewards revenue regardless of profitability.
- Options are repriced after poor performance.
- Insiders retain voting control with limited economic ownership.
- Related-party transactions are frequent.
- Board members lack relevant experience or independence.
- Share count rises persistently without corresponding value creation.

# Part VI — Challenges, Risks, and Future Prospects

## 15. Current Challenges

### Objective

Identify the most important problems the company must solve now.

### Challenge categories

- Product
- Customer adoption
- Competition
- Pricing
- Technology
- Supply chain
- Regulation
- Talent
- Execution
- Profitability
- Financing
- Reputation
- Governance

### Standard for a strong analysis

For each challenge, specify:

1. **Problem**
2. **Evidence**
3. **Root cause**
4. **Potential financial impact**
5. **Management response**
6. **Evidence the response is working**
7. **Timeline**
8. **Leading indicators to monitor**

### Challenge template

| Challenge | Evidence | Root cause | Impact mechanism | Management response | Indicator | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

### Where to find information

- Risk factors
- MD&A
- Earnings-call questions
- Customer complaints
- Competitor gains
- Employee reviews
- Regulatory actions
- Lawsuits
- Supplier commentary
- Industry data
- Short-seller reports, used as hypothesis sources
- Changes in company disclosures

### Red flags

- Management describes a structural issue as temporary.
- The same challenge appears for several years without improvement.
- Metrics needed to assess progress are discontinued.
- Management’s solution depends on unproven future products.
- The issue threatens liquidity before the solution can arrive.

## 16. Risk Analysis

### Objective

Identify how the investment thesis can fail and estimate the severity, probability, and detectability of each risk.

### Risk framework

For each risk, estimate:

- Probability
- Impact
- Time horizon
- Reversibility
- Early warning indicators
- Management control
- Existing mitigation
- Residual risk

### Suggested risk matrix

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Time horizon | Early indicator | Mitigation | Residual risk |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|---|---|

### Standard for a strong analysis

Risks should be causal and company-specific.

Weak:

> Competition could increase.

Strong:

> A large platform can bundle a comparable feature at no incremental price, which may reduce renewal rates and weaken the company’s ability to raise prices.

Distinguish:

- Thesis-breaking risks
- Earnings volatility
- Temporary setbacks
- Valuation risks
- Balance-sheet risks
- Fraud and governance risks

### Where to find information

- 10-K Risk Factors
- 10-Q updates
- Legal proceedings
- Regulatory databases
- Debt agreements
- Supplier and customer concentration disclosures
- Cybersecurity disclosures
- Competitor strategy
- Insurance and warranty disclosures
- Industry incident databases

### Red flags

- Boilerplate risk discussion with little company-specific detail.
- Risk factors change only after the problem becomes obvious.
- High-impact risks have no measurable indicator.
- The company depends on continuous external financing.
- One regulatory decision could invalidate the business model.

## 17. Growth Opportunities and Future Prospects

### Objective

Determine what the company could become and what must happen for that outcome to materialize.

### Growth vectors

- More customers in the current segment
- Higher penetration within existing customers
- Price increases
- Increased product usage
- New products
- New geographies
- New customer segments
- Cross-selling
- Distribution expansion
- Acquisitions
- Industry consolidation
- Regulatory change
- Margin improvement

### Questions

- What is the core growth engine?
- Which opportunities are proven, emerging, or speculative?
- What capabilities are required?
- What capital is required?
- Could growth damage the core business?
- Does the company have a right to win in the adjacency?
- What evidence shows customer demand?
- What is the expected timeline?
- What milestones would confirm or reject the opportunity?

### Standard for a strong analysis

Classify opportunities:

| Category | Definition |
|---|---|
| Proven | Existing product, demonstrated customer demand, repeatable economics |
| Emerging | Initial demand and some evidence of repeatability |
| Speculative | Conceptual opportunity with little operating evidence |

Do not value all three categories equally.

### Where to find information

- Product roadmap
- Research and development spending
- Customer pilots
- Hiring plans
- Capital expenditures
- Regulatory applications
- Patent filings
- Partnership agreements
- Competitor initiatives
- Investor presentations
- Industry adoption curves

### Red flags

- The future thesis depends on products not yet released.
- Every new initiative targets a different customer.
- Growth opportunities require capabilities the company has never demonstrated.
- Management highlights total market size but not customer demand.
- New products cannibalize the highest-margin existing product.

# Part VII — Research Process

## 19. Recommended Research Sequence

### Phase 1: Orientation

1. Read the latest 10-K or annual report.
2. Review the company’s product pages.
3. Listen to or read the latest earnings call.
4. Map revenue segments and customer groups.
5. Write the one-sentence business model.

### Phase 2: Historical reconstruction

1. Review at least three to five years of filings.
2. Track changes in strategy, metrics, and language.
3. Compare guidance with actual results.
4. Identify acquisitions, divestitures, and accounting changes.
5. Build a timeline of major product and management events.

### Phase 3: External validation

1. Test the product.
2. Interview customers.
3. Speak with distributors, suppliers, or implementation partners.
4. Compare competitors.
5. Read critical commentary.
6. Search regulatory, legal, and patent databases.

### Phase 4: Economics

1. Reconstruct revenue drivers.
2. Estimate unit economics.
3. Analyze margins and cash conversion.
4. Measure dilution and capital intensity.
5. Compare economics with competitors.

### Phase 5: Thesis formation

1. State the core customer value proposition.
2. Identify the moat.
3. Identify the most important challenge.
4. Define bear, base, and bull cases.
5. List disconfirming evidence.
6. Define ongoing monitoring indicators.

## 33. Source Selection Rules

The agent must prefer sources in this order:

1. Regulator filings and legally accountable disclosures
2. Company product documentation and contractual terms
3. Customer, supplier, distributor, and competitor primary evidence
4. Government, regulator, and trade-association data
5. Credible specialist research
6. Company presentations and executive interviews
7. General media
8. Forums and social media

Rules:

- Use company marketing materials to understand claims, not to validate those claims.
- Do not cite search-result snippets as evidence.
- Do not cite an aggregator when the original source is available.
- Do not treat estimated data as reported data.
- Label paywalled or inaccessible evidence.
- Prefer sources published close to the period being analyzed.
- Preserve historical sources when evaluating prior management promises.
- For each major conclusion, seek at least one source independent of the company.
- If reliable evidence is unavailable, state that directly.

## 34. Citation and Provenance Standard

Every material factual statement must have an inline citation.

A citation must point to:

- The exact filing, transcript, page, section, table, or document
- The publication date
- The period to which the fact applies

For regulatory filings, prefer a format such as:

> Company, Form 10-K, fiscal year ended YYYY-MM-DD, Item 1, p. XX.

For web sources, preserve:

- Page title
- Publisher
- Publication date
- Access date
- URL

The report must contain a source appendix and an evidence log.

No citation should be generated from memory.

## 35. Confidence Standard

Assign confidence separately to each major conclusion.

| Confidence | Standard |
|---|---|
| High | Multiple consistent primary sources, including independent validation |
| Medium | Credible evidence exists but is incomplete, indirect, or largely company-provided |
| Low | Sparse, conflicting, old, estimated, or anecdotal evidence |
| Unknown | Evidence is insufficient to form a responsible conclusion |

Do not convert confidence into false numerical precision.

- Customer value proposition

- Competitive position
- Pricing power
- Unit economics
- Capital requirements
- Management credibility
- Balance-sheet survival
- Long-term growth rate
- Long-term margin
- Share dilution
- Thesis-breaking risk

Exclude low-value detail that does not affect the investment or business thesis.

# Part X — Value Chain and Public-Company Ecosystem Analysis

## 45. Purpose

This module identifies the target company's upstream inputs, downstream routes to market, end customers, complementors, substitutes, bottlenecks, bargaining power, profit pools, and relevant public companies.

The goal is an evidence-supported economic map, not the longest possible company list.

## 46. Core Definitions

**Upstream:** Providers of raw materials, components, manufacturing, cloud infrastructure, data, software, logistics, equipment, intellectual property, energy, or specialized services used by the target.

**Company layer:** Activities performed by the target, including design, procurement, production, hosting, sales, support, billing, and monetization.

**Downstream:** Distributors, resellers, integrators, marketplaces, retailers, OEM partners, channel partners, enterprise customers, and end-market industries.

**Complementors:** Products or services that increase the target product's usefulness or adoption.

**Substitutes:** Alternative methods of solving the same customer problem.

Competitors must not automatically be classified as downstream, and suppliers must not automatically be called strategic partners.

- Sole-source component
- Scarce manufacturing capacity
- Regulatory license
- Proprietary data
- Dominant platform or cloud provider
- Critical logistics route
- Specialized talent
- Distribution gatekeeper
- Concentrated customer budget
- Standard-setting body

## 61. Structural Change

Assess:

- Vertical integration
- Outsourcing
- Reshoring or nearshoring
- Platform consolidation
- Direct distribution
- Open-source substitution
- Technology transitions
- Regulation
- Capacity expansion
- Commodity inflation
- Channel conflict
- Customer internalization

Distinguish current structure from likely future structure.

## 64. QA Checklist

- [ ] Upstream and downstream are defined relative to the target.
- [ ] Confirmed relationships are separated from category participants.
- [ ] Every direct relationship has a citation.
- [ ] Reverse-direction verification was performed.
- [ ] Each public listing was resolved.
- [ ] Subsidiaries and public parents are distinguished.
- [ ] Historical relationships are not presented as current.
- [ ] Customers are not inferred from end-market membership.
- [ ] Suppliers are not inferred from technical compatibility.
- [ ] Relationship direction is correct.
- [ ] Materiality is not assumed.
- [ ] Profit pools and bottlenecks are analyzed.
- [ ] Unverified candidates are excluded from headline findings.
- [ ] Every graph edge resolves to evidence.
- [ ] Graph and narrative relationships are consistent.
